The accounting is the crisis, the water is measurable, and the fix pencils. A data-and-ROI companion to designing the next Colorado River.

The Colorado River is having its worst water year on record in the same year its rulebook expires. Steve Kloos calls the fix "Basin 2.0" [src] — a redesign for a climate the 1922 Compact never imagined. This is the quantitative backbone for that argument: the four most-contested numbers, computed from public data, then turned into a costed, financeable portfolio.

The thesis in one line: the basin's hardest fights are accounting fights, the disputed quantities are already measurable, and the cheapest thing to buy is not new water but agreed measurement. Every figure below links to its primary source, and the data and code are downloadable, so you can confirm and extend rather than take our word.

The evidence · four measured findings

1.2–1.5MAF/yr lost to evaporation & system losses [1][2]

The loss no one was charged for

Since Arizona v. California (1964), Lower Basin evaporation and system losses have been booked to neither state and simply drained the reservoirs [src]. Charging them, as the Upper Basin proposes [src], moves over a million acre-feet of cut onto Arizona and California first. The measurement is not optional.

−1.53MAF/yr storage lost · ~24 MAF of it groundwater [1][2]

The larger loss no proposal counts

GRACE satellites show 46.3 km³ of water gone since 2002 (our computation validates the published ~52), draining ~3.5× faster now. About 24 MAF is groundwater — a Lake Mead’s worth — invisible to the surface-water accounting the Law of the River runs on.

10.4×value gap · forage $362 vs. produce $3,750 per acre-foot [1][2]

The scarcest water grows the cheapest crop

In four Lower Basin counties, forage consumes ~2.05 MAF at ~$362/acre-foot of value [src]. Cities pay 3–4× more, produce 10×. This is why compensated fallowing is the cheapest large block of water, and why conservation should be paid on measured consumptive use [src], not diversions.

2 → 41days/yr the release crosses the bass-spawning threshold [src]

A national park run by power revenue

As Lake Powell fell, the dam released warmer water. Bass-suitable days rose from ~2 to ~41 a year (11,654 USGS readings), and smallmouth bass now pass into the last humpback-chub stronghold [1][2]. Cool-mix flows work but are contested by hydropower [src]. The physics is predictable, so the trade-off can be priced.

$42B → 4.67 MAF/yr, all seven stakeholders cleared

The optimizer picks eight measures under a $50B ceiling with a hard benefit floor for every stakeholder: ~$8B of headroom, more water freed than the whole structural deficit. A $10B slice already frees 1.87 MAF and clears every stakeholder; returns diminish above $25B. Tribal infrastructure and aquifer recharge don’t enter the pure cost-optimal set — those are equity and resilience choices worth funding on their own track.

The opportunity · ROI and blue bonds

It pencils, and it can be financed by the beneficiaries

The package returns NPV +$58B, IRR ~32%, benefit-cost ~13×. The pattern is on-thesis: the cheap data-and-efficiency options carry the returns, the capital-heavy megaprojects don’t.

Can all $50B be blue bonds repaid by beneficiaries?

Yes [1][2] — and for less. The freed water (~4.67 MAF/yr) is worth ~$8.7B/yr, far above the debt service. A tiered per-acre-foot beneficiary surcharge services the bonds:

BondDebt service (5%, 30yr)Beneficiary surchargeCoverageStructure
$28B (package capital)$1.85B/yr$126–159/AF4.2–5.3×$8.7B revenue-backed, rest assessment

Surcharge across ~13 MAF of basin use · trivial for cities (>$1,000/AF), affordable for ag ($362/AF) → tier it [1][2]. Revenue-backed = options with water-sales/lease/fee cash flow; the rest are public goods repaid by the surcharge. The instrument is proven; the gap is an issuer with basin-wide assessment authority.

The coalition · who must move

The Grand Compromise: a package, not a menu

No actor can pass this alone. It works as a sequenced deal where each party contributes and each gains. Timeline: cloud seeding, conservation, and ag efficiency deliver in 1–2 years; measurement in 5–7; the Glen Canyon retrofit is the 10–15 year long-pole. The critical-path bottleneck is political consensus on the interstate conservation market, and the post-2026 guidelines are the venue.

Federal (Reclamation, Interior)

Avoids systemic collapse, endless litigation, and having to impose draconian cuts unilaterally. Gets an administrable long-term framework.

gives: funding + authority

Lower Basin cities & ag

Cities get reliability without $2,500/AF desal. Farmers get paid for measured savings and keep the value through leasing rather than losing it to a mandated cut.

gives: measured conservation

Upper Basin states

Evaporation finally booked, a Compact Call averted, and cloud-seeding + measurement investment aimed at their headwaters.

gives: participation, not a blank check

Tribes

Full parties to the measurement ledger, settlement infrastructure funded, and leasing authority that turns ~1 MAF of unused senior water into income.

gives: senior-right certainty

Environmental / Grand Canyon

A downstream temperature target, cool-mix flows protected, and phreatophyte restoration — the ecosystem gets a seat in the operating rules.

gives: legitimacy + science

Capital (Burnt Island lens)

The measurement, conservation-market, and efficiency layers are the high-BCR, revenue-bondable core — innovation over legacy capital, financed by a blue bond.

gives: the money, de-risked
Interrogate this

Confirm it, don’t take our word

Every number here is computed from public data on public-cloud credits and checked against the literature by an independent adversarial review (four of six clusters confirmed, two minor precision revisions applied). The inputs are open:

  • Groundwater (M3): NASA JPL GRACE-FO mascons — PO.DAAC dataset, validated vs. the published ~52 km³ [src].
  • Ecology (M4): the raw temperature record, USGS gauge 09380000.
  • Agriculture (M2): live USDA Cropland Data Layer + OpenET.
  • Evaporation (M1): USGS measured evaporation + Powell area-capacity.
  • Method, code, costings, and the ROI / blue-bond model are in the project data room (gs://steps-colorado-river): per-module numbers, the portfolio optimizer, the finance model, and the adversarial-review transcripts.

Assumptions worth pressure-testing: water valued at $1,500/AF (range $800–2,500 modeled); the groundwater share uses the literature 65% pending a direct GLDAS decomposition; the GRACE basin polygon is approximate; solution costs are grounded priors, Monte-Carlo-stressed, not engineering estimates. All are stated in the data, not hidden.
