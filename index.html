The accounting is the crisis.

The basin’s hardest fights are accounting fights, and the water is already measurable. We computed the four most-contested quantities from public data.

The Colorado River is having its worst water year on record in the same year its rulebook expires. The 2007 guidelines lapse on December 31, 2026, the seven states have missed every deadline, and Washington is threatening to impose a plan. Every technical fight underneath is really about who absorbs a 3 to 3.5 million-acre-foot gap between what the law promised and what the river delivers.

So we did the measurement. Each number below was computed from public datasets on cloud credits and validated against the published literature. The through-line: measurement, not new water, is the cheapest thing the basin can buy.

The loss no one was charged for

Since a 1964 Supreme Court ruling, Lower Basin evaporation and system losses have been booked to neither state and simply drained the reservoirs. Charging them to the Lower Basin, as the Upper Basin proposes, moves well over a million acre-feet of cut onto Arizona and California before anything else. The fight over who pays is legitimate. The measurement is not optional.

−1.53MAF / year of total water storage lost · ~24 MAF of it groundwater

The larger loss no proposal counts

GRACE satellites show the basin has lost 46.3 cubic kilometres of water since 2002 (validated against the published 52), draining ~3.5× faster now than at the start. About 24 MAF of that is groundwater, a Lake Mead’s worth, invisible to the surface-water accounting the Law of the River runs on. A deal can stabilize the reservoirs while the basin keeps draining the reserve underneath it.

10.4×value gap · forage $362 vs. produce $3,750 per acre-foot

The scarcest water grows the cheapest crop

In four Lower Basin counties, forage covers ~353,000 acres and consumes ~2.05 MAF, roughly an eighth of the river, at ~$362 per acre-foot of value. Cities pay three to four times more, produce ten times more. This is why compensated forage fallowing is the cheapest large block of water in the basin, and why conservation should be paid on measured consumptive use, not on diversions.

2 → 41days / year the release crosses the bass-spawning threshold, pre-2015 vs. now

A national park managed by power revenue

As Lake Powell fell, the water released below Glen Canyon Dam warmed. Bass-suitable days rose from about two a year to forty-one (11,654 USGS readings), and smallmouth bass now pass the dam into the last stronghold of the humpback chub. The thermostat on a national park is currently set by hydropower revenue, not by the ecological threshold. The physics is predictable, so the trade-off can be priced.

Five recommendations
1

Book the evaporation

Allocate the 1.2–1.55 MAF of evaporation and seepage explicitly, and fund a current satellite ledger to measure it. Stop absorbing it in storage.

serves Upper & Lower Basin
2

Price conservation on wet water

Pay for measured consumptive use, not diversions, and make compensated forage fallowing the first, best-paid block of cuts. Let farmers capture the value by leasing.

serves agriculture & cities
3

Account for committed groundwater

Stand up a basin-wide ledger separating physically-present from already-committed groundwater, and net out the pumping-to-baseflow lag before counting it as a buffer.

serves rural & every state
4

Set a downstream temperature target

Operate Glen Canyon releases to hold below the bass-spawning threshold in the spawning window, and price the avoided loss against foregone power on stated numbers.

serves environment & power
5

Make measurement the shared currency

Fund and jointly govern a public measurement ledger — evaporation, consumptive use, groundwater, temperature — and write the rules to settle on it. The one investment that de-risks every dispute.

the entry point for capital

The three things the science says to do are not Upper-versus-Lower positions. They are where every proposal is silent.
